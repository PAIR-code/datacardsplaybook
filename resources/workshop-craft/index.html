Workshop: Participatory Activities for Dataset Documentation

March 5, 2021

Presented by Andrew Zaldivar, Mahima Pushkarna, and Daniel Nanas (Google Research)

The Data Cards Playbook workshop at ACM FAccT 2021 walks through a selection of activities and frameworks in 01 Ask and 02 Inspect. Running through four sessions, the workshop alternates between introductions to concepts and interactive breakout sessions with facilitators.

Participants will use a carefully crafted vignette to explore and collaborate on a unique template for transparent dataset documentation.

By the end of this workshop, participants will have a clear understanding of the foundational knowledge, design principles, and commitments required to create dataset transparency reports in their respective domains.

CRAFT Workshop

Full proposal

In the context of developing, deploying and using datasets, a critical aspect in maintaining a good relationship between stakeholders (from data practitioners to the public) is for them to understand the importance of transparency to that relationship. Without that, it is impossible to secure an ethically appropriate measure of social transparency in dataset practices, as well as good faith efforts by stakeholders to embrace all aspects of responsible transparency. A lack of consensus and evolution of transparency efforts could pose major risks to society as these dataset documentation practices serve as the foundation for data technologies that impact the quality of people's lives.

Transparency, which we define as sharing information about system behavior (casual or observed) and organizational process that is concise, understandable, and articulated with plain language explanations, is captured in the form of boundary objects such as documentation and summarized reports. These reports typically provide descriptive, structural and sometimes statistical information about a dataset. But details that go beyond metadata, including provenance, representation, usage and fairness-informed evaluations—context stakeholders require for making responsible decisions about dataset use—are often not included or are unavailable. As a consequence of poor dataset documentation standards and inconsistent practices, stakeholders are ill-equipped to make informed decisions around dataset release and adoption.

In the past few years, researchers and practitioners have developed frameworks to standardize the process for documenting datasets. Datasheets for Datasets [1] is one such attempt that draws critical analogies from the automobile industry, clinical trials in medicine, and electronics industry to improve the relationship between creator and consumer stakeholders. An ideal adoption of Datasheets supplies creators with guided thinking around dataset creation that prioritizes accountability and societal impact, and consumers with knowledge to decide on appropriate use of dataset in high-stakes domains. Dataset Nutrition Labels [2] is another such framework that is inspired by nutrition facts labels and label warnings utilized in product safety, pharmaceuticals and material safety to refine information down to the essentials. This type of diagnostic framework gives data practitioners the ability to swiftly assess and interrogate the contents of a dataset. Finally, Model Cards [3], a short-form document for trained machine learning models that is complementary to Datasheets for Datasets, includes a dedicated section for detailing underlying context of training and evaluation datasets. Together, along with FactSheets [4] for AI services, Data Statements [5] for natural language processing datasets, ABOUT ML [6] for machine learning systems, and other frameworks represent current transparent and accountable dataset documentation practices.

As demonstrated by these aforementioned frameworks, the standards and demands of dataset documentation are developing. However, we find that these approaches are fragmented and unable to scale due to the nuances of each dataset or AI system they describe. These developments are in part due to how challenging it is achieve transparency to the point at which stakeholders can establish a socratic understanding of a dataset—that is, encouraging healthy dialogue between stakeholders based on asking and answering questions to stimulate critical thinking about a dataset, and to draw out ideas and underlying presuppositions that lead to responsible development, deployment and use of a dataset. Beyond its non-trivial nature, reaching this level of transparency is burdened by dataset documentation generally falling under the responsibility of a single person. Dataset creation is the work of many—critical and nuanced knowledge distributed across people, in time, and in processes—and that should also be reflected when writing documentation. These challenges are further exacerbated by the unrealized needs and requirements of every stakeholder involved in a dataset's lifecycle, hinged on necessary yet usually nonexistent shared taxonomy and vocabulary. Difficult to remedy through a single framework, these fractures make it demanding to apply current dataset documentation practices in responsible decision making.

In this session, participants will learn about and use the Data Cards Playbook (“Playbook”) to establish a foundation for producing, publishing, and measuring transparent documentation of datasets, addressing current limitations in this practice to obtain more desirable documentation outcomes. The Playbook is a framework-agnostic, human-centered, participatory design approach to dataset documentation we created that offers a consistent way for stakeholders to extract knowledge distributed across the many individuals involved in creating datasets. In turn, this knowledge acquisition process encapsulates the unique information needs of consumers and reviewers of a dataset, beyond metadata. The Playbook provides guidance on gathering this information with usefulness, thoughtfulness and measurability in mind.

Through a set of group activities, engaging discussions, and readily available resources, the Playbook is designed for virtual and in-person workshop facilitation with methods that assist participants in creating, completing, and customizing templates for different types of dataset documentations. This Playbook can be used to define and produce nuanced and transparent documentation for any dataset type, for any documentation framework. By the end of this session, participants will acquire a clear understanding of the actions and commitments required to create dataset transparency reports that are accessible to diverse stakeholders, with foundational knowledge and design principles, as well as artifacts created throughout this session, necessary to apply this approach in their respective domain.

Using a carefully crafted vignette to facilitate in-depth and multi-faceted explorations of complex dataset documentation creation, participants will work in small groups with an appointed facilitator. First, groups will identify stakeholders and their information needs in relation to our vignette material. This activity will involve participants developing an understanding of our taxonomy of stakeholders to align on agents—stakeholders who use, evaluate, or determine how the dataset is used. Participants will then draft details on what their dataset documentation will need to capture and how it might be organized. This is accomplished through a set of brainstorming group exercises that aid participants in framing and formulating questions. To allow participants the flexibility to consume the material of the playbook without necessarily participating in the activities, all presentations will occur in a central location, and groups will work in break-out rooms.

Participants will continue by assembling a straw-man proposal. This straw-man will allow groups to critically examine questions written in previous sessions so as to find gaps and opportunities for proper documentation. Subsequently, participants will engage in a critique and refine their draft using a provided baseline set of dimensions to measure and steer their content outline for a diverse set of agents. Thereafter, participants will consider a plan for producing answers to their agreed-upon questions, followed by a recap on all the design principles introduced throughout this session.

At the end of this session, each group will share and discuss their draft with all participants, hosting a broader conversation on the current state of transparency led by presenters. We will then, collectively, debrief on all the concepts and ideas introduced throughout this session and conclude with thoughts and ideas raised by participants on how we can further evolve the state of responsible dataset transparency.
